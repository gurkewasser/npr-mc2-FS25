Introduction

Sentiment analysis, also known as opinion mining, is a subfield of natural language processing (NLP) that focuses on identifying and categorizing the emotional tone conveyed in a piece of text. It aims to determine whether the sentiment expressed is positive, negative, neutral, or falls into other nuanced categories like anger, joy, or fear. This technique is widely used in applications such as social media monitoring, customer feedback analysis, market research, and even in gauging public opinion on political events or products.

Dataset

For this challenge we used the Amazon Polarity dataset. The Dataset contains 4 million reviews.

Data Fields

  • 'title': a string containing the title of the review - escaped using double quotes (") and any internal double quote is escaped by 2 double quotes (""). New lines are escaped by a backslash followed with an "n" character, that is "\n".
  • 'content': a string containing the body of the document - escaped using double quotes (") and any internal double quote is escaped by 2 double quotes (""). New lines are escaped by a backslash followed with an "n" character, that is "\n".
  • 'label': either 1 (positive) or 0 (negative) rating.

Data splitting strategy

To make the large Amazon Polarity dataset more manageable and suitable for experimentation in a semi-supervised learning setup, we created a balanced and downsized subset of 4,000 samples. This subset was split as follows:

  • Validation Set: 800 samples (400 positive, 400 negative)
  • Labeled Training Set: 400 samples (200 positive, 200 negative)
  • Unlabeled Training Set: 2800 samples (1400 positive, 1400 negative)

Each subset maintains a 50/50 class balance to ensure fairness during training and evaluation.

The splits were saved in .parquet format to the /data directory for efficient storage and faster I/O operations during development.

This setup enables easy experimentation with semi-supervised learning approaches, where only a small fraction of the data is labeled.

To see the progress, the splitting was done in splitting_notebook

Model Training: Fine-Tuning & Transfer Learning

This section defines the approaches and techniques used to train the sentiment classification models. All model training code is organized in standalone script files, namely:

  • fine-tuning.py
  • transfer-learning.py
  • classic-baseline.py

These scripts rely on the Hugging Face transformers library, which provides robust tools for working with pre-trained language models and training them for specific downstream tasks such as sentiment analysis.

Pretrained Model

For both training strategiesfine-tuning and transfer learningthe same base model is used: all-MiniLM-L6-v2, a lightweight, efficient transformer designed for sentence embeddings. This model is pretrained on a large and diverse text corpus, excluding the Amazon Polarity dataset used in this challenge to prevent any risk of data leakage.

The model is loaded with a sequence classification head, which consists of a simple feed-forward neural network on top of the transformer output. This classification head is trained to predict the sentiment label of a given input text.

Transfer Learning vs. Fine-Tuning

The key distinction between the two approaches lies in which parts of the model are updated during training:

  • Transfer Learning
    In this setup, the pretrained transformer layers are frozen. Only the classification head is trained on the labeled sentiment data. This approach is useful when computational resources are limited or when labeled data is sparse, as it reduces the risk of overfitting.

  • Fine-Tuning
    In contrast, the fine-tuning process updates all model parameters. The entire modelincluding the transformer layers and the classification headis trained end-to-end on the sentiment data. Fine-tuning generally yields better performance when sufficient labeled data is available.

Despite this difference in training strategy, the overall training pipeline (data preparation, tokenization, evaluation metrics, etc.) is kept consistent to allow for a fair comparison of results.

Tokenization & Data Preparation

The model-specific tokenizer from Hugging Face is used to preprocess text data. Tokenization includes truncating or padding sequences to a fixed length and converting tokens to input IDs and attention masks suitable for transformer models.

Hierarchically nested training datasets are used to simulate varying availability of labeled data and assess the impact of data size on performance.

Training Configuration

All models were trained using the following standardized hyperparameters unless otherwise stated:

  • Optimizer: AdamW
  • Learning Rate: 2e-5
  • Sequence Length: 128 tokens
  • Batch Size: 32
  • Learning Rate Warmup Steps: 500
  • Number of Epochs: 50, with early stopping applied (patience = 6) to halt training when no improvement is observed on the evaluation metric.

Training was conducted on GPU infrastructure to handle larger batches efficiently and reduce training time. The use of early stopping ensures that the model avoids overfitting while making full use of the available training capacity when necessary.

Baseline

As a classical baseline, we used a TF-IDF vectorizer with unigrams and bigrams (ngram_range=(1,2)) and a maximum of 5000 features. A Logistic Regression classifier (max_iter=1000) was trained on the vectorized text.

Each sample's text was constructed by concatenating its title and content. Performance was evaluated on a fixed validation set using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, with results plotted across increasing training sizes.

Transfer Learning

For transfer learning, we fine-tuned the sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 modela compact and efficient transformer optimized for sentence embeddings.

Key steps:

  • Model & Tokenizer: Loaded all-MiniLM-L6-v2 via Hugging Face Transformers for sequence classification.
  • Preprocessing: Combined title and content fields into a single text string. Used padding and truncation (max_length) during tokenization.
  • Data Handling: Converted training and validation datasets from .parquet format to Hugging Face Dataset objects.
  • Training Setup: Utilized the Trainer API with:
    • Early stopping,
    • Evaluation metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, F1),
    • Gradient accumulation and learning rate scheduling.
  • Evaluation: Tracked and logged performance over different training sizes to compare with classical baselines.

This setup leverages a sentence-transformer model to improve classification while being computationally efficient.

Fine-Tuning

For fine-tuning, I trained a Transformer-based sequence classification model on a custom dataset using Hugging Face's Trainer API.

Key steps:

  • Model & Tokenizer: Loaded a pre-trained Transformer model (e.g., AutoModelForSequenceClassification) with num_labels=2 for binary classification.
  • Preprocessing: Combined title and content into a single text field. Applied tokenization with padding and truncation.
  • Data Handling: Read .parquet files using pandas, then converted them to Hugging Face Dataset objects with proper formatting for PyTorch (input_ids, attention_mask, label).
  • Training Setup: Used TrainingArguments and the Trainer API with:
    • Early stopping,
    • Epoch-based evaluation, logging, and checkpointing,
    • Metric tracking (accuracy, precision, recall, F1),
    • Weights & Biases (wandb) for logging and visualization.
  • Evaluation: Trained and evaluated the model across multiple training dataset sizes, saving performance metrics to CSV files for further analysis.

This pipeline allowed me to automate experiments efficiently and log fine-tuning runs with consistent reproducibility and evaluation.

Why Accuracy is Used

Im using accuracy as the evaluation metric because our dataset is balanced, meaning all classes are equally represented. In such cases, accuracy reliably reflects the model's performance, as no class dominates the predictions.

What the Plot Shows

The plot displays the accuracy vs. train size for three different methods:

  • Classical: Improves with more data but saturates around 77%.
  • Fine-tuning: Achieves the highest accuracy (~94%) even with small train sizes, indicating strong performance and good generalization.
  • Transfer: Starts low and improves slightly but performs worse overall than fine-tuning and classical methods.

This shows that fine-tuning is the most effective method in this scenario, especially with limited training data.

Justification for Choosing a Training Size > 400

Although a large portion of the maximum achievable accuracy is already reached at a training size of around 400particularly with the fine-tuning methodall three methods still show small but consistent improvements beyond this point:

  • Fine-tuning: Accuracy continues to increase slightly and stabilizes around 94.5%, indicating high model capacity and effective learning with a larger dataset.
  • Classical: While performance stabilizes, this level is only reached at around 400. A larger dataset helps reduce fluctuations and improves robustness.
  • Transfer: Although this method shows diminishing or fluctuating trends beyond 450, it only reaches stable performance around 350400.

Conclusion: A training size greater than 400 is chosen to fully leverage model capacity, enhance generalization, and ensure performance stability across methodseven if marginal gains decrease.

Conclusion

This analysis compared classical machine learning, transfer learning, and fine-tuned transformer models across varying training set sizes. The results clearly show that fine-tuning a pre-trained transformer model significantly outperforms both classical methods and simple transfer learningachieving over 94% accuracy even with modest amounts of training data.

While classical models offer a reasonable baseline and transfer learning provides a lightweight alternative, fine-tuning proves to be the most effective in balancing performance and data efficiency. Thus, for similar text classification tasks, investing in fine-tuning transformer models is strongly recommended, particularly when high accuracy is a priority.
